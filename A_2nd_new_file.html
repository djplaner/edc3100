Do you care?

When it comes to what you learn in this course and the grade you get at the end of the course, the most important factor is and should be you.

In particular, the biggest factor is whether you care. Dave Cormier - a Canadian University educator - writes about this being the first principle of learning. It's a perspective I have a lot of time for. If a learner doesn't care about what they are learning, it's going to be more difficult/unlikely that they will learn.

As a teacher, I have attempted to make it easier for you to care about this course, but I can't make you. That's your choice.

USQ, personalised learning and your responsibilities

Another reason why you are the most important person in this course connects with USQ's strategic plan. One of the pillars of that plan is personalised learning. That in part is described this way

Through innovation USQ harnesses emerging technologies to enable collaborative teaching and individualised learning for its students regardless of their location or lifestyle.

Exactly what this means is open to debate. As with much in education the phrase "personalised learning" has many meanings. The Wikipedia page on personalised learning covers many of them. The definition that I like and have tried to achieve in the design of this course is from the National Educational Technology Plan from the US Department of Education, which is described on Wikipedia as

personalized learning is defined as adjusting the pace (individualization), adjusting the approach (differentiation), and connecting to the learner's interests and experiences. Personalization is broader than just individualization or differentiation in that it affords the learner a degree of choice about what is learned, when it is learned and how it is learned. The rhetoric is often phrased in terms of learning 'any time, any where or any place'. This may not indicate unlimited choice, since learners will still have targets to be met. However, it may provide learners the opportunity to learn in ways that suit their individual learning styles and multiple intelligences.

What does this mean?

In this course, this doesn't mean that everything about the course has been personalised for every single learner. Instead it means that the course has been designed to give you the flexibility to choose where, when and what you will focus your learning on. The role of the teaching staff will be to help provide that flexibility. If you find the course inflexible, let us know.

Digital native or digital immigrant


byWilliam M Ferriter

Are you a digital native? Or are you a digital immigrant?

This distinction is sometimes understood as anyone born before 1970 is a digital immigrant. i.e. they don't really understand digital technologies. If you didn't grow up with it you can never really get it.

This is a silly question. The concept of digital natives and immigrants is completely and utterly unhelpful and it is also not true.

Some evidence for this statement includes:

  1. my example;

    I was born before 1970. A few years ago I had to explain to my teenage daughter why she was such a loser for being on MySpace and that Facebook was actually the place to be. I build software and web applications. ICT is firmly embedded in my pedagogy. I am not a digital immigrant simply because of my age.

    In the last couple of days I've had to help out my 8 and 10 year old sons with various aspects of their computer use. They've grown up in a house with access to a lot of ICT. They do not inherently know how to use ICTs.

  2. the literature; and,

    Bennett et al (2008) analyse the digital native literature and the evidence on which it is based. They conclude

    The picture beginning to emerge from research on young people’s relationships with technology is much more complex than the digital native characterisation suggests. While technology is embedded in their lives, young people’s use and skills are not uniform. There is no evidence of widespread and universal disaffection, or of a distinctly different learning style the like of which has never been seen before. We may live in a highly technologised world, but it is conceivable that it has become so through evolution, rather than revolution. Young people may do things differently, but there are no grounds to consider them alien to us. Education may be under challenge to change, but it is not clear that it is being rejected. (p. 783)
  3. the originator's perspective.

    The apparent originator of these terms explains on his personal website that this understanding of his argument is mistaken.

References

Bennett, S., Maton, K., & Kervin, L. (2008). The "digital natives" debate: A critical review of the evidence. British Journal of Educational Technology, 39(5), 775–786. doi:10.1111/j.1467-8535.2007.00793.x

You can teach an old dog new tricks

What type of mindsets do you have?

Test your mindset - do you have a fixed or growth mindset?

What about when you think about ICTs? Do you have a fixed or growth mindset?

Based on past experience, there's a good chance that you think you and technology don't get along. It's not something you're comfortable with. Especially not when you're trying to teach a class. You might even be scared of technology.

There are almost certainly going to be times during this course when you get really, really frustrated with technology. You may even think that you'll never get it.

Between the work of Carol Dweck on fixed versus growth mindsets (watch the video below or check out this blog post that does a good job of summarising her work) and work on brain plasticity the old adage that you can't teach an old dog new tricks is false. Old dogs can learn new tricks, and the learning of new tricks is good for them.

i.e. you can learn how to effectively use ICT to enhance and transform your pedagogy.

It just takes time, effort and a bit of encouragement. If you're having problems, don't think you can't, just think "not yet" (the video below will explain "not yet")

Can you be a digital renovator?


byCity of Boston Archives

By the end of this semester, you will probably be going on Professional Experience and will be expected to use ICT to enhance and transform your teaching. It's also likely that the quality and quantity of ICT available to you in your classroom will remind you of the classroom to the right. The technology you have available will be limited, probably won't work very well, and may well require a fair bit of effort to get something effective done.

This is a challenge that many folk in EDC3100 will face when they go on PE. It's often their biggest concern when they go on PE. Will the ICT be good enough to use?

Even though USQ typically provides a much greater level of ICT, it's a problem that I face teaching EDC3100. I've even started using the term "concrete lounge" to describe the feeling I get when using some of the USQ technologies.

You as a digital renovator

This is where the idea of you as a digital renovator enters the picture.

No-one would be silly enough to just live with a concrete lounge. Instead they would seek to change it, to renovate. If you were faced with a concrete lounge, at the very least you would be looking for some pillows or cushions. You might even engage in more radical renovations.

The digital environment is not meant to be a concrete lounge. Digital technologies can be manipulated and renovated. We'll pick up on this again in week 1.

My hope is that as we progress through the course you will begin to think of yourself as a digital renovator.
